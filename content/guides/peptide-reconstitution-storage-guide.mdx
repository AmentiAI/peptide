# How to Reconstitute and Store Research Peptides

Research peptides are supplied as lyophilized (freeze-dried) powders to maximize shelf stability during shipping and storage. Before use in any research application, they must be reconstituted (dissolved) in an appropriate sterile solvent. This guide covers the complete process from reconstitution to storage to disposal.

## What is Lyophilization?

Lyophilization (freeze-drying) removes water from the peptide solution by freezing it and then reducing the surrounding pressure to allow the frozen water to sublimate directly from solid to vapor. The result is a dry, stable powder that maintains peptide integrity for months to years at cold storage temperatures.

Key benefits of lyophilized peptides:
- Extended shelf life (12–24+ months when refrigerated)
- Stable during shipping at ambient temperatures
- Accurately dosed (the vial label weight reflects actual peptide content)

## Equipment You Need

Before reconstituting any research peptide, gather:
- **Bacteriostatic water (BW)** — for reconstitution and multi-use vials
- **Sterile water for injection** — alternative for single-use applications
- **Insulin syringes (1mL)** — U-100 (100 units = 1mL) for easy dosing
- **Alcohol swabs** — for sterilizing vial tops
- **Labels** — for recording concentration and date of reconstitution

**Critical note on solvent choice:** Use bacteriostatic water (0.9% benzyl alcohol) for any vial you will access multiple times. Bacteriostatic water prevents bacterial growth for up to 28–30 days after opening. Regular sterile water should only be used for single-use applications as it does not have antimicrobial protection.

## Step-by-Step Reconstitution Protocol

### Step 1: Prepare Your Work Surface
- Use a clean, flat surface
- Wipe down with 70% isopropyl alcohol
- Work near a lamp or bright light to see the powder clearly

### Step 2: Inspect the Vial
- Confirm the vial contains a solid cake or powder (not liquid — if liquid, the lyophilization failed)
- Check the color (most peptides are white; GHK-Cu is blue when dissolved; some others may be slightly off-white)
- Verify the vial is sealed and the stopper is intact

### Step 3: Calculate Your Target Concentration
Decide on your target concentration based on the dose you'll be administering:

**Common calculation:**
- 2mg peptide + 2mL bacteriostatic water = **1mg/mL (1000mcg/mL)**
- 2mg peptide + 4mL bacteriostatic water = **0.5mg/mL (500mcg/mL)**
- 5mg peptide + 5mL bacteriostatic water = **1mg/mL (1000mcg/mL)**

**Dose calculation example:**
- You want 250mcg of BPC-157
- Your concentration is 1000mcg/mL
- 250mcg ÷ 1000mcg/mL = **0.25mL (25 units on a U-100 insulin syringe)**

### Step 4: Draw Up the Bacteriostatic Water
- Wipe the bacteriostatic water vial top with an alcohol swab
- Allow to dry 10–15 seconds
- Draw the calculated volume of BW into your syringe

### Step 5: Reconstitute the Peptide
- Wipe the peptide vial top with a fresh alcohol swab
- Allow to dry
- **Insert the needle into the peptide vial at an angle** (45–60°)
- Slowly inject the BW **along the inside wall of the vial**, not directly onto the powder
- This gentle flow down the glass prevents mechanical denaturation of the peptide

### Step 6: Dissolve the Peptide
- **Do NOT shake the vial** — this can cause foaming and peptide degradation
- Gently swirl or roll the vial between your palms
- Allow the vial to sit for 2–5 minutes if the powder doesn't dissolve immediately
- Repeat gentle swirling until completely clear solution is achieved

### Step 7: Label the Vial
Write on the label:
- Peptide name
- Concentration (mcg/mL or mg/mL)
- Date of reconstitution
- "Use by" date (28–30 days from reconstitution)

### Step 8: Refrigerate Immediately
Place the reconstituted vial in the refrigerator (2–8°C) immediately after use. Do not leave at room temperature.

## Common Reconstitution Mistakes to Avoid

**❌ Injecting water directly onto the powder:** Creates a turbulent mixing that can mechanically damage peptide structure. Always inject along the vial wall.

**❌ Shaking the vial:** Foaming introduces air bubbles and shear stress. Always gently swirl only.

**❌ Using room-temperature water on sensitive peptides:** Use refrigerated bacteriostatic water for GH peptides which can be more temperature-sensitive.

**❌ Using regular sterile water for multi-use vials:** Without bacteriostatic protection, bacterial contamination can occur within 24 hours. Always use bacteriostatic water.

**❌ Storing reconstituted peptides in a freezer:** This is appropriate for lyophilized powder but reconstituted peptides should NOT be frozen — this can cause precipitation and degradation.

**❌ Ignoring the "use by" date:** Reconstituted peptides degrade over time. 28–30 days is the maximum for bacteriostatic water reconstitutions.

## Signs of Peptide Degradation

Watch for these signs that a reconstituted peptide may have degraded:
- **Cloudy or turbid solution** (should be clear after full dissolution)
- **Precipitate or particles** in solution
- **Unusual color change** (other than the expected GHK-Cu blue)
- **Strong or unusual odor**

If any of these are present, do not use the peptide in research. Discard and reconstitute a fresh vial.

## Long-Term Storage of Lyophilized Peptides

For peptides that will not be used for 3+ months:
- Store in a freezer at -20°C
- Vacuum-sealed or nitrogen-flushed storage extends shelf life further
- Avoid storing near the freezer door (temperature fluctuates with opening/closing)
- Each freeze-thaw cycle reduces peptide viability — thaw only what you need

## Disposal

Follow your institution's or local guidelines for disposal of research chemicals. Generally:
- Reconstituted peptide vials should be disposed of as lab waste
- Needles and syringes in sharps containers
- Do not pour peptide solutions down household drains in large quantities
